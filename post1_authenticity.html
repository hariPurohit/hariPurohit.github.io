Authenticity - Impulsivity

Authenticity as peddled by the masses implies that a person has to be "in-tune" with their impulses. But in philosophy, the term "authenticity" has a different meaning. Authenticity is defined as owning one's actions. But unlike the more familiar term of autonomy, authenticity also means that one is in harmony with their desires. Although the occasionaly post-modernist would argue that the later definition has corrupted young minds, leading to ever-growing individualistic greed we see in our societies, I would say this is not the case. Authenticity as I experience it is similar to what early german phenomenologists (Kierkegaard and Heideggar) have talked about. According to them, authenticity as not just acting in accordance to one's feelings in the current moment, while completing disregarding the constraints of their past. Rather, authenticity is ownership of one's actions. Actions that "were", actions that "are", and actions that will be.

With this as the starting point, I would like to argue that authenticity refers to a moment-to-moment commitment to actions. Before I begin, I put forward a few constraints. First, the term 'moment-to-moment' is deliberately left unspecified by me. What constitutes a temporal moment is a hotly debated topic and one that i do not wish to divulge into. Hence, this term would just refer to what a person considers as a moment, as loosely specified by what they are currently feeling. Second, thoughts and action are cooupled by the ideomotor principle. Simply put, our thoughts would translate into actions (behavior), in majority of the cases. Third, committing to action does not mean that a person always is 'moving about', and for the lack of a better term, not staying still or idle. Commitment simply means their actions can reasonably serve as a reflection of their intentions.

Now that the constraints are set up, let me begin. Authenticity is ownership. Phenomenologically, an "authentic action" feels exactly like two puzzle pieces fitting perfectly. This feeling of a 'perfect fit' could represent a lack of mental conflict. Contrast this to an "in-authentic action" which feels like the thrashing of waves against a rocky shore. An unstoppable force, against an immovable object. The thoughts and actions are in clear conflict with each other.
